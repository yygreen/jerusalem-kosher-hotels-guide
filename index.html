Choosing a Kosher Hotel in Jerusalem

For a frum family, almost nothing on a hotel booking page decides the trip. These five questions do.

Most hotel reviews rank rooms by thread count and lobby marble. For an observant family flying into Jerusalem, what matters is a different checklist — one you usually only learn after a Shabbos spent walking forty minutes each way because the "central" hotel turned out to be outside the eruv. Here is that checklist.

1. Whose hechsher is in the kitchen?

"Kosher hotel" in Jerusalem isn't one thing. Kitchens span Badatz Eida HaChareidis, Badatz Agudat Yisrael, Mehadrin by a named rav, and Rabbanut Mehadrin. The real question isn't "is it kosher?" — it's which hechsher, whether the meat is also glatt/chalak, and whether bread and produce are pas yisroel and bishul yisroel. A hotel that just says "kosher" is telling you to ask.

2. Is there a real Shabbos elevator — and what floor are you on?

A lobby sign doesn't help if your room is on the 14th floor and the Shabbos cycle only services even floors, or if one elevator serves 300 rooms. Ask how many run on Shabbos mode and what floor you're on. (And if your family follows the stricter ruling and takes the stairs, a low floor is the whole decision.) See which Jerusalem hotels run a Shabbos elevator.

3. Is the hotel inside the eruv?

Jerusalem's central eruv is large but doesn't cover every neighborhood. Inside it you can carry a key and push a stroller; outside it, Shabbos shrinks to whoever can walk unencumbered. Confirm the hotel's specific street — not just "Jerusalem has one." More on the eruv and Jerusalem hotels.

4. How far — on foot — to minyan, mikveh, and the Kotel?

Map distances are measured by car; yours will be measured at stroller pace in Shabbos shoes. Ask walking minutes to the nearest shul with minyanim at your times, a mikveh (on-site is ideal), and the Old City / Kotel.

5. What changes on Yom Tov?

Pesach, Sukkos, and the Yamim Noraim turn Jerusalem into the most in-demand frum destination on earth, and the rules shift — a Sukkos stay needs its own kosher sukkah; the Yamim Noraim go largely car-free and on foot. These dates sell out months ahead, so it helps to know how far in advance to book for each Yom Tov.

6. So where do you actually start?

Once you know what to ask, the fastest way to narrow Jerusalem's hotels is by what your trip turns on. If the deciding factor is neighborhood — chareidi immersion in Geulah, central and walkable near Haneviim, or full-service at the entrance to the city in Romema — start with a neighborhood-by-neighborhood guide to where to stay. If you are traveling with children and need connecting rooms, a family apartment, or a pool, the family-hotels rundown sorts that out. And if the walk to the Kotel matters most, there is a list of the kosher hotels closest to the Kotel, ranked by foot-time.

The short version: when comparing two Jerusalem hotels, the deciding questions are whose hechsher, how many Shabbos elevators and what floor, inside the eruv or not, walking minutes to minyan/mikveh/Kotel, and what changes on Yom Tov.
